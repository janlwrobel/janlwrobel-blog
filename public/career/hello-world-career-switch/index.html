Hello World: My Journey to Switching Careers into IT#

From Manufacturing to IT Support#

A few years ago, I was assembling aircraft seats and working in quality control for medical equipment.

Today I work as an IT Support Engineer, troubleshooting Microsoft 365 issues, Outlook failures, OneDrive sync problems, printers that refuse to print five minutes before meetings, and all the other chaos that comes with real-world IT.

This is the story of how I got here.

Growing Up Between Mechanics and Technology#

I was born in Poland and grew up near Słupsk, in a small village called Smołdzino.

Like many people from my generation, I grew up around computers from an early age. Games, hardware, Windows reinstalls, random drivers downloaded from suspicious websites — the classic experience.

At the same time, I was always interested in engineering and mechanics, which eventually led me to study Mechanical Engineering at the Koszalin University of Technology.

That degree gave me something I still use daily in IT:

problem-solving.

Because whether you are fixing a machine or debugging a broken Outlook profile, the process is surprisingly similar.

Something is broken.
You gather information.
You isolate the cause.
You test solutions.
You try not to make things worse.

Working in Engineering#

Before IT, I worked in several engineering and manufacturing roles in the UK.

At Honeywell, I worked as a Power Press Setter and later in mechanical assembly environments focused on aircraft seating systems.

Between those roles, I also worked at Olympus Corporation in quality control for medical endoscopy equipment.

Those jobs taught me attention to detail, documentation, procedures, and responsibility.

In manufacturing and medical environments, mistakes matter.

You cannot just “hope it works.”

That mindset transferred into IT better than I expected.

The Moment IT Started Pulling Me In#

Even while working in engineering, technology slowly became more than just a hobby.

I spent more and more time:

  • troubleshooting computers,
  • learning networking basics,
  • building PCs,
  • experimenting with Linux,
  • reading forums and documentation,
  • and eventually writing simple scripts.

Looking back, part of this interest was already present during my university years.

My specialization at the Koszalin University of Technology focused on Engineering Applications of Computers, which covered areas related to computer systems, software, and technical computing. Even though I continued into mechanical and manufacturing roles after graduation, that technical foundation stayed with me.

Over time, I realised I enjoyed solving technical problems on computers more than working with mechanical systems.

That was the moment I started seriously considering a career switch into IT.

The Software Engineering Bootcamp#

To make the career transition real, I joined a Software Engineering bootcamp focused on Python.

Programming was not completely new to me. During my engineering studies, I had already worked with languages such as C, C++, and later C#, as software and automation are closely connected with modern engineering.

Over the years, most of that knowledge faded while working in manufacturing roles, but the interest in technology never disappeared.

When the UK government launched funded retraining programmes, I decided to take the opportunity seriously and enrolled in the bootcamp.

The experience was challenging, but it completely changed the way I approached problem-solving and technology.

I learned:

  • Python,
  • debugging,
  • Git,
  • software development fundamentals,
  • and how to think more like a developer.

More importantly, it gave me confidence that I could realistically build a career in IT.

Starting in IT Support#

Eventually, I landed my first role as an IT Support Engineer.

It was not software engineering.

But honestly, I think starting in support was one of the best things that could have happened to me.

Helpdesk exposes you to real environments very quickly.

Not tutorial environments.
Not clean lab setups.
Real businesses.

You learn:

  • Microsoft 365,
  • Active Directory,
  • Exchange,
  • networking basics,
  • permissions,
  • troubleshooting,
  • user communication,
  • and how unpredictable production systems really are.

You also learn that printers are immortal and feed on human suffering.

Building a Homelab#

Outside work, I started building my own homelab using old Dell OptiPlex machines running Proxmox VE.

That became another major learning step.

I began experimenting with:

  • virtualization,
  • Docker containers,
  • Linux servers,
  • reverse proxies,
  • Cloudflare Tunnel,
  • monitoring,
  • backups,
  • self-hosted services,
  • and automation.

Most importantly:

things constantly broke.

And honestly, that is where most real learning happens.

Why I Started This Blog#

One thing annoyed me while learning IT.

Too much content online feels fake.

Everything is “Top 10 DevOps Tips.”

Everything is “Ultimate Kubernetes Guide.”

But almost nobody talks about what actually breaks in real environments.

So this blog is different.

This is not a theory blog.

This is:

  • real troubleshooting,
  • real mistakes,
  • real fixes,
  • real infrastructure,
  • real helpdesk stories,
  • real homelab experiments.

Sometimes things here will fail spectacularly.

Good.

That is how real IT works.

Where I Am Now#

I still do not consider myself “finished.”

IT changes too fast for that.

Right now I work in IT support while continuing to build my skills in:

  • infrastructure,
  • automation,
  • Linux,
  • scripting,
  • virtualization,
  • Microsoft 365,
  • and eventually DevOps-oriented technologies.

This blog is part of that journey.

Not a polished success story.

A real one.

TL;DR#

I went from mechanical engineering and factory work to IT support, homelabs, scripting, and infrastructure.

This blog documents that transition honestly — including the failures, troubleshooting, and lessons learned along the way.

Because real IT is messy.

And that is exactly why I enjoy it.

If you are also trying to switch careers into IT, build a homelab, or simply learn new technical skills, hopefully some of the things I share here will help you avoid at least a few of the mistakes I made along the way.
